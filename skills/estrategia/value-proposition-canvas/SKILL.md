# Value Proposition Canvas

## When to Use This Skill

Use this skill when you need to:
- Map your value proposition against specific customer segments
- Identify customer jobs, pains, and gains to inform product and messaging decisions
- Align your product features with what customers actually care about
- Articulate why your offer is the best solution for a specific audience

**DO NOT** use this skill for competitive analysis, pricing strategy, or brand identity work. This is for structured value proposition mapping using the canvas framework.

---

## Core Principle

Your value proposition is not what you think your product does. It is the overlap between what your customer needs and what you uniquely deliver. The canvas reveals that overlap.

---

## Phase 1: Brief

### Required Inputs

| Input | What to Ask | Default |
|-------|------------|---------|
| **Product/service** | "What do you offer?" | No default — must be provided |
| **Target customer** | "Who is this for? Be specific." | No default — must be provided |
| **Current messaging** | "How do you currently describe your product?" | Whatever they share |
| **Customer research** | "Have you talked to customers? What do they say?" | Limited or anecdotal |
| **Competitors** | "Who else solves this problem?" | General awareness |

**GATE: Confirm inputs before building the canvas.**

---

## Phase 2: Customer Profile (Right Side of Canvas)

### Customer Jobs

Identify what the customer is trying to accomplish:

```
## Customer Jobs

**Functional Jobs (tasks they need to complete):**
1. [Job 1 — e.g., "Send professional invoices to clients"]
2. [Job 2 — e.g., "Track which invoices are paid and overdue"]
3. [Job 3 — e.g., "Manage cash flow across multiple clients"]

**Social Jobs (how they want to be perceived):**
1. [Job — e.g., "Look professional and established to clients"]
2. [Job — e.g., "Be seen as organized and on top of finances"]

**Emotional Jobs (how they want to feel):**
1. [Job — e.g., "Feel confident about business finances"]
2. [Job — e.g., "Reduce stress around getting paid"]
```

### Customer Pains

```
## Customer Pains

**Frustrations:**
1. [Pain — e.g., "Creating invoices from scratch takes too long"]
2. [Pain — e.g., "Chasing late payments is awkward and time-consuming"]

**Risks/Fears:**
1. [Pain — e.g., "Worried about looking unprofessional"]
2. [Pain — e.g., "Fear of cash flow gaps sinking the business"]

**Obstacles:**
1. [Pain — e.g., "Don't know what to include on an invoice"]
2. [Pain — e.g., "No system for tracking who owes what"]
```

### Customer Gains

```
## Customer Gains

**Required Gains (must-haves):**
1. [Gain — e.g., "Invoices sent and paid reliably"]

**Expected Gains (standard expectations):**
1. [Gain — e.g., "Professional-looking invoices"]
2. [Gain — e.g., "Easy tracking of payment status"]

**Desired Gains (would love to have):**
1. [Gain — e.g., "Automated payment reminders"]
2. [Gain — e.g., "One-click invoice creation"]

**Unexpected Gains (wow factors):**
1. [Gain — e.g., "Cash flow forecasting based on invoice data"]
```

**GATE: Review the customer profile before mapping the value side.**

---

## Phase 3: Value Map (Left Side of Canvas)

### Products and Services

List what you offer that addresses the customer jobs.

### Pain Relievers

For each customer pain, show which feature or aspect of your product relieves it.

### Gain Creators

For each customer gain, show how your product creates or amplifies it.

### Fit Analysis

```
## Value Proposition Fit

| Customer Pain/Gain | Your Solution | Fit Strength |
|--------------------|---------------|-------------|
| [Pain 1] | [Feature/benefit] | Strong / Moderate / Weak |
| [Pain 2] | [Feature/benefit] | Strong / Moderate / Weak |
| [Gain 1] | [Feature/benefit] | Strong / Moderate / Weak |
```

Highlight:
- **Strong fits** — these are your core selling points
- **Weak fits** — gaps to address in product development or acknowledge in messaging
- **Unaddressed jobs/pains** — opportunities or boundaries to communicate

---

## Phase 4: Polish

### 1. Value Proposition Statement

Distill the canvas into a single statement:

```
For [target customer] who [job to be done],
[Product name] is the [category] that [key benefit].
Unlike [alternative], we [key differentiator].
```

### 2. Messaging Hierarchy

Rank the value proposition elements by importance:
1. Primary message (the #1 pain you relieve)
2. Secondary messages (2-3 supporting gains)
3. Proof points for each message

### 3. Application Guide

Show how to use the canvas output for:
- Sales page headlines and copy
- Ad messaging and targeting
- Product development priorities
- Customer support scripts

## Example 1: Bookkeeping SaaS for Shopify Sellers

**Brief:**
- Product: Cloud bookkeeping tool with native Shopify + Amazon integration and a human CPA review each month.
- Target customer: Solo Shopify sellers doing $100K–$500K/year.
- Current messaging: "Accounting made easy for e-commerce."
- Customer research: 6 interviews with Shopify sellers; top pain = "I do bookkeeping quarterly in a panic."
- Competitors: Bench (generalist), A2X (software-only), local bookkeepers (no e-commerce expertise).

**Customer Profile:**

Jobs:
- Functional: categorize Shopify payouts; reconcile Amazon FBA fees; produce clean P&L at tax time.
- Social: look professional to accountant/investor; not be "the founder who lost all receipts."
- Emotional: feel in control of cash; stop dreading tax season.

Pains:
- Frustration: Shopify payouts are net of fees and refunds — manual reconciliation takes 4–6 hrs/month.
- Risk: missing a write-off because of missing transactions; IRS penalty.
- Obstacle: QuickBooks imports raw payout lumps, not per-order detail.

Gains:
- Required: accurate monthly P&L; clean books at year-end.
- Expected: integrations that "just work"; phone/email support.
- Desired: CPA review included, not add-on.
- Unexpected: cash flow forecast based on Shopify sales trend.

**Value Map:**

Pain Relievers:
- Native Shopify payout splitter (turns one deposit into per-order revenue + fees + refunds) → kills the 4–6 hr reconciliation.
- A2X-style Amazon FBA fee parser → eliminates missing-writeoff risk.
- Monthly CPA review included in $299 plan → removes tax-season dread.

Gain Creators:
- 14-day rolling cash-flow projection from Shopify sales data → desired/unexpected gain.
- "Books-closed-by-the-5th" SLA → expected gain, over-delivered.

**Fit Analysis:**

| Customer Pain/Gain | Your Solution | Fit |
|--------------------|---------------|-----|
| 4–6 hrs manual payout reconciliation | Shopify payout splitter | Strong |
| FBA fee confusion | A2X-style parser | Strong |
| Tax-season dread | Included CPA review | Strong |
| Expected: integrations that work | Native Shopify + Amazon | Strong |
| Unexpected: cash flow forecast | 14-day rolling projection | Moderate (MVP) |
| Emotional: look professional | Clean P&L export | Weak — address in messaging, not product |

**Value Proposition Statement:**

> For solo Shopify sellers doing $100K–$500K a year who dread quarterly bookkeeping panic, [Product] is the e-commerce-native bookkeeping tool that closes your books by the 5th of every month with CPA review included. Unlike Bench, we speak Shopify and Amazon fluently; unlike A2X, we include the human review.

**Messaging Hierarchy:**
1. Primary: "Books closed by the 5th. CPA-reviewed. Every month."
2. Secondary: "Native Shopify payout splitter — no more 4-hour reconciliation weekends."
3. Secondary: "Amazon FBA fees, decoded. See real margin per SKU."
4. Proof: "Trusted by 200+ Shopify sellers doing $20M+ combined GMV."

## Example 2: Small-Group Personal Training Studio

**Brief:**
- Product: Small-group personal training (4-person classes) + optional 1:1, $200/mo group or $100/session.
- Target customer: Austin adults 30–50 who want structured training but feel Orangetheory is too generic and CrossFit too intimidating.
- Competitors: Orangetheory (full-service class franchise), Iron Tribe CrossFit, ATX Strength (boutique premium).

**Customer Profile:**

Jobs:
- Functional: build strength / improve body composition without injury; stick to a consistent schedule.
- Social: be "the 40-year-old who looks 30"; belong to a gym people respect.
- Emotional: feel capable and strong; stop feeling self-conscious in commercial gyms.

Pains:
- Frustration: Orangetheory's one-size-fits-all heart-rate model ignores their knee issues.
- Risk: getting hurt doing CrossFit Olympic lifts without technique coaching.
- Obstacle: don't know how to program their own training; tried and plateaued.

Gains:
- Required: show up, be coached, leave better.
- Expected: clean facility, booking app that works, coach remembers their name.
- Desired: personalized programming inside a group class (not solo 1:1 cost).
- Unexpected: progress tracking that shows measurable strength gains quarter over quarter.

**Value Map:**

Pain Relievers:
- Individualized program written for each client, executed within the 4-person class → solves "OTF is generic."
- Coach-led movement standards with scaling per client → removes CrossFit injury anxiety.
- Quarterly strength testing + progression review → solves the plateau / "am I actually improving?" obstacle.

Gain Creators:
- 4:1 coach ratio at group pricing → desired gain delivered.
- Private Slack community for clients + monthly "Ask the coach" session → social belonging without CrossFit cult feel.
- Per-client strength log visible in app → unexpected gain.

**Fit Analysis:**

| Customer Pain/Gain | Your Solution | Fit |
|--------------------|---------------|-----|
| OTF generic / ignores injuries | Individualized programming in group | Strong |
| CrossFit injury risk | Coach-led standards + scaling | Strong |
| Plateau / no measurable progress | Quarterly testing + app log | Strong |
| Required: show up and be coached | 4:1 ratio, booked class format | Strong |
| Social: belong to a gym people respect | Slack community + progress shares | Moderate |
| Emotional: feel capable | Strength metrics + coach praise | Moderate |

**Value Proposition Statement:**

> For Austin adults 30–50 who want real strength training without CrossFit risk or Orangetheory sameness, [Studio] is the small-group training studio where every client gets a program written for them — inside a 4-person class. Unlike Orangetheory, we program around your body; unlike CrossFit, we coach every rep.

**Messaging Hierarchy:**
1. Primary: "Your own program. In a class of four."
2. Secondary: "Quarterly strength tests. Proof you're getting stronger."
3. Secondary: "Coached every rep. Scaled to your body. Zero ego."
4. Proof: "Average client adds 40 lbs to their back squat in 12 weeks."

## Anti-Patterns

- **Listing features instead of jobs** — "We have a dashboard" is a feature. "Track all invoices in one place" is a job.
- **Guessing customer pains** — if you have not talked to real customers, the canvas is fiction. Base it on research.
- **Trying to fit everything** — you cannot relieve every pain and create every gain. Focus on the strongest fits.
- **One canvas for multiple segments** — different customer segments have different jobs, pains, and gains. Create a separate canvas per segment.
- **Never updating the canvas** — customer needs evolve. Revisit quarterly.

---

## Recovery

- **No customer research:** Recommend 5 short interviews with existing or potential customers. Provide interview questions to use.
- **Too many customer segments:** Start with the highest-value or largest segment. Complete one canvas fully before starting another.
- **Weak fit across the board:** This is valuable insight — it may mean the product needs repositioning or the target customer needs to change.
- **User cannot articulate their differentiator:** Use the canvas to find it. The overlap between customer needs and what competitors miss is the differentiator.
