# Lead Magnet

## When to Use This Skill

Use this skill when you need to:
- Create a free downloadable resource to grow an email list (checklist, cheat sheet, guide, template, swipe file)
- Write the landing page copy that convinces someone to opt in for that resource
- Build a complete lead-magnet-plus-landing-page package from a single topic or pain point
- Replace a generic "subscribe to my newsletter" with a specific, high-value opt-in incentive

Do not use this skill for full-length ebooks, paid products, course content, or sales pages for paid offers. This is for free, short-form lead magnets (1-3 pages) and the opt-in page that promotes them.

---

## Core Principle

Every lead magnet should solve one specific problem so clearly that the reader thinks "if the free stuff is this good, the paid stuff must be incredible." That reaction — not the email capture — is what drives eventual conversions, which is why thin or generic resources hurt the funnel even when they lift opt-in rates.

---

## Lead Magnet Type Selector

Choose one type. Each has different strengths depending on the audience and topic.

| Type | Best For | Creation Speed | Perceived Value | Length |
|------|----------|---------------|-----------------|--------|
| **Checklist** | Step-by-step processes, launch sequences, routines | Fastest | Medium | 1 page |
| **Cheat Sheet** | Reference material, formulas, shortcuts, quick-look guides | Fast | Highest | 1-2 pages |
| **Mini-Guide** | Teaching a framework, explaining a method, building authority | Medium | High (authority) | 2-3 pages |
| **Template** | Fill-in-the-blank documents, scripts, frameworks | Fast | Highest (practical) | 1-2 pages |
| **Swipe File** | Proven examples to model (emails, headlines, CTAs, ad copy) | Medium | High | 2-3 pages |

**Default recommendation:** if the user is unsure, recommend a checklist for action-oriented topics or a cheat sheet for reference-oriented topics. These two have the best effort-to-conversion ratio.

For full skeletons, fill-in templates, and rules per type (including bonus types: quiz, challenge, webinar replay, resource library), read `references/templates.md`.

## Phase 1: Strategy

Gather four pieces of information before writing anything. No strategy, no lead magnet.

### The Four Strategy Questions

1. **Topic and audience pain point** — What specific problem does this solve, and for whom?
2. **Lead magnet type** — Checklist, cheat sheet, mini-guide, template, or swipe file?
3. **Desired action after download** — What should the reader do next? (book a call, buy a product, join a community, reply to an email, start a trial)
4. **Voice and brand tone** — Professional, casual, bold, friendly, data-driven?

### Strategy Brief Template

Present this to the user before proceeding:

```
## Lead Magnet Strategy Brief

**Topic:** [specific problem being solved]
**Target audience:** [who this is for]
**Pain point:** [the frustration or gap this addresses]
**Lead magnet type:** [checklist / cheat sheet / mini-guide / template / swipe file]
**Title (working):** [benefit-driven title]
**Desired next action:** [what you want them to do after reading]
**Tone:** [professional / casual / bold / friendly / data-driven]
```

See `references/examples.md` for filled-in briefs (content creator, freelancer).

**Gate:** do not proceed to Phase 2 until the user confirms or adjusts the strategy brief.

## Phase 2: Create the Lead Magnet Content

Write the actual downloadable resource. This is the content the reader receives after opting in.

### Content Rules (apply to every type)

- 1-3 pages maximum. If it takes longer than 10 minutes to consume, it is too long.
- One problem, one solution. Do not cover three topics. Solve one thing completely.
- Action-first language. Every item starts with a verb (Write, Send, Check, Remove, Set up).
- No filler paragraphs. Every sentence must teach, instruct, or provide a reference.
- Include a "quick win" in the first 3 items — something the reader can do in under 5 minutes and see immediate results.

### Skeleton (all types)

Every type follows the same shape: **Title → "Who This Is For" (one sentence) → Core Content → "What to Do Next"**. The Core Content block is what changes.

### Quick Reference — Core Content per Type

| Type | Core Content | Key Feature |
|------|--------------|-------------|
| Checklist | 3-7 named steps, each 3-5 checkboxes (15-25 total) + Quick Reference table | Checkbox items start with action verbs |
| Cheat Sheet | Quick Reference Table (5-15 rows) + 3-7 Rules + Common Mistakes | Scannable table is the centerpiece |
| Mini-Guide | Named framework with 3 parts + Real-World Example walkthrough | Framework gets a memorable label |
| Template | Usage instructions + blank template + filled-in example | Filled example shows what "done" looks like |
| Swipe File | Usage instructions + 3-5 categories, each with 2-4 examples + "Why it works" notes | Always add "adapt, never copy verbatim" |

For the full skeleton, field labels, rules, and anti-patterns per type, read `references/templates.md`. For a complete worked example ("The Ultimate Content Repurposing Checklist"), see `references/examples.md`.

### Mini-Example (Checklist fragment)

```
# The 23-Point Content Repurposing Checklist

## Who This Is For
Solo content creators who publish on 2+ platforms and want to stop
writing everything from scratch.

## Step 1: Pick Your Source Piece
- [ ] Choose your best-performing content from the last 30 days
- [ ] Confirm the core idea can be explained in one sentence
- [ ] Identify at least 3 supporting points in the piece
```

**Gate:** present the lead magnet content to the user. Do not proceed to Phase 3 until the user approves or requests changes.

## Phase 3: Write the Landing Page Copy

Write the opt-in page copy that convinces visitors to download the lead magnet. This page has one job: get the email address.

### Seven Elements, In Order

1. **Headline** — benefit-driven, specific, ideally with a number. Default formula: "The [Specific] [Type]".
2. **Subheadline** — one sentence adding specificity (number, timeframe, or audience).
3. **Benefit Bullets** (3-5) — format: **[Feature] so you can [benefit]**.
4. **CTA Button Text** — describe the action ("Send Me the Checklist"), never "Submit" or "Sign Up".
5. **Objection Handler** — address the #1 hesitation. Default: "Will I get spammed?"
6. **Social Proof** — testimonial, number proof, or authority proof (placeholders OK).
7. **Repeat CTA** — same button + a re-framing line, below social proof.

### Mini-Example (headline + one bullet + CTA)

```
Headline:   The 23-Point Content Repurposing Checklist for Solo Creators
Bullet:     -> A "building blocks" extraction method that turns one blog
               post into 5 pieces in under 60 minutes
CTA:        [Send Me the Checklist]
```

For all four headline formulas, full bullet sets, the objection-handler table, social proof options, and two complete landing-page copy examples, read `references/landing-page.md`.

**Gate:** present the landing page copy to the user. Do not finalize until the user approves the headline, bullets, CTA, and objection handler.

## Anti-Patterns

Avoid these failure modes when creating lead magnets or landing page copy:

- **Lead magnet too long.** A 20-page PDF is an unfinished ebook, not a lead magnet. Keep it to 1-3 pages — completion is what produces the "if free is this good..." reaction.
- **Gating generic content.** If it's already the top Google result, it isn't worth an email. Offer a specific framework, process, or curated resource that saves real time.
- **Vague landing copy.** "Download our free guide to grow your business" converts around 2%; "The 23-Point Checklist That Turns One Blog Post Into a Week of Content" converts above 15%. Specificity is the variable.
- **Multiple CTAs on the landing page.** The page has one job. Extra links leak attention. One CTA, repeated twice.
- **Skipping the objection handler.** Every visitor has a reason not to opt in. The handler isn't optional.
- **Writing for everyone.** "A guide for entrepreneurs" is too broad. "A checklist for solo content creators publishing on 2+ platforms" speaks directly to one person.
- **Promising the paid offer inside the free resource.** The lead magnet must be valuable on its own. A 3-page sales pitch disguised as a checklist destroys trust.
- **Hype language.** "Revolutionary," "game-changing," "the secret to," "unlock your potential" are empty. Use numbers, timeframes, concrete outcomes.

## Recovery

- **User does not know their topic or pain point:** Ask (1) What do you sell? (2) What question does your audience ask you most often? (3) What is the first thing you help a new client do? The answers surface the topic.
- **User cannot choose a type:** Default to checklist for processes/sequences, cheat sheet for reference material. Give the recommendation with a one-sentence rationale.
- **Lead magnet content feels too thin:** Add a "Common Mistakes" section or a "Quick Reference Table." High-value, low-word-count additions.
- **Landing page copy feels generic:** Go back to the strategy brief and check if the pain point is specific enough. "Grow my business" → "Get 3 new clients this month without cold calling."
- **User wants a longer resource (5+ pages):** Redirect to a mini-guide, warn that over 3 pages hurts completion. Recommend breaking into a 1-page cheat sheet + follow-up email sequence.
- **If 3 attempts at the headline fail:** Stop iterating. Ask the user for their own rough version and polish that instead of generating from scratch.
